Model Comparison Analysis

Topic: Predicting Customer Renewal in B2B SaaS

1. Objective

This analysis compares two models for predicting whether a customer will renew in the next quarter:

  • Classification Tree
  • K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)

The goal is to determine which model better matches the actual target values and to explain why the result occurred.

2. Dataset Overview

The dataset contains 10,000 customer accounts and 22 columns. The target variable is renewed_next_quarter.

Class Count Ratio
1 = Renewed 5,138 51.38%
0 = Churned 4,862 48.62%

The target variable is relatively balanced. This matters because the model results are less likely to be distorted by class imbalance. Therefore, performance differences between models are more meaningful.

The tree also showed a reasonable gap between training and test performance, suggesting that it generalized better than a strongly overfit model.

Final conclusion: The Classification Tree won overall.

The tree achieved better Accuracy, Precision, and F1-score. KNN only performed better in Recall.

6. Why Did the Classification Tree Win?

6.1 Strong threshold-based structure

The tree model identified clear threshold-based rules, especially through variables such as:

  • usage_drop_pct_30d
  • tenure_months
  • nps_score
  • feature_adoption_score

These are not random variables. They reflect real business logic:

  • Usage drop indicates disengagement.
  • Tenure reflects relationship maturity.
  • NPS captures customer satisfaction.
  • Feature adoption reflects product stickiness.

6.2 Better fit for non-linear segmentation

The Classification Tree can naturally capture nonlinear if-then rules. For example, renewal may depend on one variable only when another variable crosses a certain threshold. A tree can represent this directly, but KNN cannot explain it in the same structured way.

6.3 More suitable for business interpretation

The tree does not only predict outcomes; it also explains why customers are classified as high-risk or low-risk. That makes it more useful for business action and customer success strategy.

7. Why Was K = 9 the Best KNN?

7.1 K = 1 clearly overfit

K = 1 produced a training accuracy of 1.0000, which means the model memorized the training data. However, its test performance was much lower. This is a classic overfitting pattern.

7.2 Larger K reduced noise

As K increased, the model became less sensitive to individual noisy observations. This improved test performance.

7.3 K = 9 gave the best balance

K = 9 produced the strongest KNN result. It reduced noise enough to improve generalization, but not so much that the prediction became overly averaged.

8. Why Did KNN Still Lose?

KNN is a distance-based model. It asks: Which customers are similar to each other?

The Classification Tree asks: What rules separate renewal from churn?

This dataset appears to be driven more by rule-based decision boundaries than by simple customer similarity. In other words, it is more important to know whether a customer crosses a critical threshold than whether that customer is close to another customer in feature space.

That is why KNN improved with better K selection, but still failed to surpass the tree.

10. Which Metric Matters Most?

If the company wants to identify as many at-risk customers as possible, Recall is important because missing a true churn-risk customer is costly.

However, for overall model selection, F1-score is more balanced because it considers both Precision and Recall. In this comparison, the final decision was made based on the overall balance of performance, and the Classification Tree had the best F1-score.

Therefore:

  • Business intervention perspective: Recall matters a lot
  • Overall model selection perspective: F1-score is the most reasonable summary metric

11. Final Interpretation

The Classification Tree clearly outperformed KNN overall. This means the renewal problem in this dataset is better explained by structured business rules than by customer similarity.

Put differently, the data suggests that renewal depends on whether customers cross important thresholds, not simply on whether they resemble nearby observations.

Final answer: Yes, the Classification Tree won.
